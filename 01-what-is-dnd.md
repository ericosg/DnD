# What Is D&D? — A Guide for Video Game & Board Game Players

## The Elevator Pitch

D&D is a collaborative storytelling game where one person (the **Dungeon Master / DM**) describes the world and controls all the non-player characters (NPCs) and monsters, and everyone else plays a single character (a **Player Character / PC**). There's no board you *must* use, no strict turn order outside of combat, and no "winning." The goal is to have fun telling a story together.

### How It Compares to What You Already Know

| Concept | HeroQuest | Baldur's Gate 3 | D&D 5th Edition |
|---|---|---|---|
| Who controls the world? | The quest book / Evil Wizard player | The game engine | The DM (a real person, improvising) |
| Combat | Grid-based, simple moves | Grid/turn-based, complex | Grid optional, turn-based, flexible |
| Character creation | Pick a hero card | Pick race/class, level up | Same idea — but on paper, with more freedom |
| What can you attempt? | Only what the rules allow | Only what the game coded | **Literally anything you can describe** |
| Story | Fixed quest | Branching but pre-written | Completely emergent — shaped by player choices |

That last row is the big one. In D&D, if you want to try to convince the dragon to let you ride it instead of fighting it, you can *try*. The DM decides how hard that is, you roll dice, and the story unfolds.

## Key Terms You Need to Know

### The People at the Table

- **DM (Dungeon Master):** Runs the game. Describes scenes, plays NPCs/monsters, adjudicates rules. Think of them as the game engine + narrator + referee.
- **PC (Player Character):** Your character. You control one.
- **NPC (Non-Player Character):** Everyone else in the world. The DM plays all of them.
- **Party:** Your group of PCs, working together.

### The Core Mechanic: The d20

Almost everything in D&D works like this:

1. You say what you want to do: *"I want to pick the lock."*
2. The DM decides if it needs a roll and tells you what kind: *"Make a Dexterity (Thieves' Tools) check."*
3. You roll a **20-sided die (d20)** and add your relevant modifier.
4. If your total meets or beats the **Difficulty Class (DC)**, you succeed.

That's it. That's the core loop. Everything else is flavor on top.

> **BG3 comparison:** This is exactly what happens under the hood in Baldur's Gate 3. You've seen the d20 roll across the screen. The difference is that in tabletop D&D, *you* describe what you're attempting, and the DM decides the DC — there's no pre-programmed list of actions.

### Dice Notation

You'll hear things like "roll 2d6" or "1d8+3." Here's how to read it:

- **d20** = a 20-sided die
- **2d6** = roll two 6-sided dice and add them together
- **1d8+3** = roll one 8-sided die, then add 3

The standard D&D dice set: **d4, d6, d8, d10, d12, d20** (and a percentile d10 for d100 rolls).

## What Is a "Setting"?

A **setting** is the world your game takes place in. Think of it like choosing between playing Skyrim vs. Lord of the Rings vs. Game of Thrones. Same basic medieval-fantasy DNA, but different geography, history, gods, and tone.

Common D&D 5e settings include:

- **Forgotten Realms** — The "default." Baldur's Gate is set here. Sword Coast, Waterdeep, Baldur's Gate the city — all Forgotten Realms. If your DM doesn't specify, it's probably this.
- **Eberron** — More magicpunk, noir, post-war. Trains powered by elementals. Warforged (living robots).
- **Ravenloft** — Gothic horror. Vampires, cursed domains, dread.
- **Homebrew** — The DM made it up. Very common and totally valid.

**What to ask your DM:** *"What setting are we playing in?"* This tells you what kind of world your character grew up in, which helps you build a backstory.

## What Is a "Backstory"?

Your backstory is your character's history *before the adventure starts*. It doesn't need to be a novel — a few sentences is fine, especially for your first game. It answers:

- **Where are you from?** A small village? A big city? The wilderness?
- **What did you do before adventuring?** Soldier? Criminal? Scholar? Farmer?
- **Why are you adventuring now?** Revenge? Curiosity? Money? Running from something?
- **Do you have any connections?** Family? A mentor? An enemy?

### Why It Matters

- It gives the DM material to weave your character into the story.
- It gives *you* a framework for making decisions ("What would my character do?").
- It helps you connect with the other PCs ("Oh, you're from Baldur's Gate too?").

### A Good First-Timer Backstory Template

> **[Name]** grew up in **[place]**. They were a **[occupation/background]** until **[inciting event]** pushed them to seek a life of adventure. They are motivated by **[goal or drive]** and struggle with **[flaw or fear]**.

Example: *"Kael grew up on a farm outside Waterdeep. He was a soldier in the city guard until his unit was wiped out by gnolls. Now he wanders, looking for purpose and haunted by survivor's guilt."*

That's enough. You can always add more later as the game inspires you.

## One Last Thing: The #1 Rule

> **The DM has final say on rules.** If the DM says something works differently than what you read, go with the DM. The game is about fun, not rules-lawyering. You can ask for clarification ("Hey, I thought that spell worked this way — am I reading it wrong?") but never argue. The DM is juggling a lot.

Your Baldur's Gate experience is genuinely helpful — you already understand advantage/disadvantage, ability checks, spell slots, and action economy at an intuitive level. The jump to tabletop is smaller than you think.
