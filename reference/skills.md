# All 5e Skills — Complete Reference

Every skill, what it does, when the DM calls for it, and practical examples.

---

## How Skills Work

```
Skill check = d20 + ability modifier + proficiency bonus (if proficient)
                                     + proficiency bonus AGAIN (if Expertise)
```

**Example calculation at level 1** (proficiency bonus = +2):

| Situation | Formula | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Non-proficient, ability modifier +2 | d20 + 2 | d20 + 2 |
| Proficient, ability modifier +3 | d20 + 3 + 2 | d20 + 5 |
| Expertise, ability modifier +3 | d20 + 3 + 4 | d20 + 7 |
| Non-proficient, ability modifier -1 | d20 - 1 | d20 - 1 |

To calculate your own modifier for any skill: take your ability modifier for that skill's ability score, then add your proficiency bonus if proficient (or double proficiency if you have Expertise).

## Strength Skills

### Athletics (STR)
**What it covers:** Any feat of raw physical exertion — climbing, swimming, jumping beyond normal limits, grappling, and resisting being grappled or shoved.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You try to climb a slippery cliff face
- You swim against a strong current
- You try to grab and hold a creature (grapple)
- Someone tries to shove you and you resist
- You try to jump across a chasm that's beyond your normal jump distance
- You hang from a ledge and try to pull yourself up

**In practice:** The only STR skill. STR-based characters (Fighters, Barbarians, Paladins) will use this often. DEX characters can sometimes convince the DM to use Acrobatics instead for climbing or balance situations — but grappling is always Athletics.

## Dexterity Skills

### Acrobatics (DEX)
**What it covers:** Balance, agility, and gymnastic maneuvers. Staying on your feet in tricky situations, landing gracefully, and performing acrobatic stunts.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You run across a sheet of ice without falling
- You balance on a tightrope or narrow ledge
- You try to land gracefully after a fall or jump
- You attempt to flip over an enemy or slide under a table
- You try to escape a grapple using agility instead of brute force (DM's discretion — Acrobatics vs. their Athletics)
- You perform a gymnastic maneuver to reach a high window

**In practice:** The DEX alternative to Athletics. When a situation calls for grace rather than power, this is your skill. Very useful in dungeon environments with precarious terrain. Popular proficiency for Rogues, Monks, and Rangers.

### Sleight of Hand (DEX)
**What it covers:** Fine motor control, pickpocketing, planting objects on someone, hiding something on your person, and any delicate hand manipulation.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You try to pickpocket someone (vs. their passive Perception)
- You try to plant a note or object in someone's pocket without them noticing
- You palm a small object from a table while talking to someone
- You try to cheat at a card game
- You conceal a weapon on your body
- You try to tie or untie a knot quickly and quietly

**In practice:** Not used as often as Stealth or Perception, but when it comes up, it's usually high-stakes — stealing a key off a guard, planting evidence, hiding a weapon before entering a restricted area. Especially useful for Rogues, Bards, and any character with a thief or spy concept.

### Stealth (DEX)
**What it covers:** Moving silently, hiding from sight, blending into shadows or crowds, and avoiding detection.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You try to sneak past guards
- You hide behind cover during combat (e.g., Cunning Action: Hide for Rogues)
- You move silently through a corridor
- You try to follow someone without being noticed (tailing)
- You approach a sleeping creature without waking it
- You try to blend into a crowd to lose a pursuer

**How hiding works in combat:**
1. You use your action or bonus action (if available, e.g., Cunning Action) to Hide
2. Roll Stealth vs. enemies' passive Perception
3. If you beat it, you're hidden — your next attack has advantage
4. Attacking (hit or miss) reveals your position

**In practice:** One of the most-used skills in the game. Rogues with Expertise can reach absurd Stealth bonuses. Essential for any character built around ambush, infiltration, or scouting. Armor with stealth disadvantage (most medium and all heavy armor) significantly hurts this skill.

## Constitution Skills

*Constitution has no skills.* It's used for:
- **Saving throws** (resisting poison, enduring extreme cold/heat, maintaining concentration on spells)
- **Hit point calculations** (CON modifier x level added to your HP)
- Rare ability checks like forced marching, holding your breath, or going without sleep

## Intelligence Skills

### Arcana (INT)
**What it covers:** Knowledge of magic, magical traditions, spells, magical creatures, mystical symbols, enchanted items, and the planes of existence.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You examine a magical glyph on a door — what school of magic is it?
- You encounter a strange magical effect — do you recognize it?
- You find a potion or magical item — can you identify it?
- You try to recall knowledge about a magical creature's weaknesses
- You encounter a portal — do you understand how it works?

**In practice:** The "do I know about this magical thing?" skill. Useful in high-magic campaigns. Wizards, Artificers, and Warlocks benefit most due to their high INT or magical focus.

### History (INT)
**What it covers:** Knowledge of historical events, wars, civilizations, legendary figures, ancient kingdoms, and past cultures.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You enter a ruin — do you know what civilization built it?
- An NPC mentions an ancient war — what do you know about it?
- You see a heraldic symbol — do you recognize the family or kingdom?
- You find a historical artifact — what's its significance?

**In practice:** Comes up when the DM wants to feed the party lore. More useful in lore-heavy campaigns. INT-based, so Wizards and Artificers get the most mileage.

### Investigation (INT)
**What it covers:** Active deduction, logical reasoning, searching for specific things, and figuring out how mechanisms work. The "Sherlock Holmes" skill.

**The crucial distinction — Investigation vs. Perception:**
- **Perception** (WIS) = You *notice* that something is off. "There's a draft coming from that wall."
- **Investigation** (INT) = You *figure out* what's going on. "There's a hidden mechanism here — if I push this brick, the wall opens."

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You search a room for hidden compartments, false bottoms, or secret doors (after you've noticed something is off)
- You examine a trap to figure out how to disarm it
- You look for clues at a crime scene — what happened here?
- You read a coded message and try to decipher it
- You try to determine how a mechanical device works
- You search through documents for a specific piece of information

**In practice:** Investigation comes up often in dungeon crawls and mystery scenarios. Pairs well with high Perception — you notice things and then analyze them. Useful for Rogues, Wizards, Artificers, and any investigative character.

### Nature (INT)
**What it covers:** Knowledge of plants, animals, terrain, weather patterns, and natural cycles. The "book knowledge" version of the natural world (as opposed to Survival, which is practical experience).

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You encounter a plant — is it poisonous or medicinal?
- You see tracks from an unusual creature — what kind of animal made them? (Identifying the creature is Nature; following the tracks is Survival)
- The weather is changing — do you understand what's coming?
- You enter a new biome — what dangers should you expect?

**In practice:** Niche. Druids and Rangers use it most. Comes up more in wilderness campaigns.

### Religion (INT)
**What it covers:** Knowledge of gods, religious practices, holy symbols, undead, cults, sacred texts, and divine hierarchies.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You see a holy symbol — which god does it represent?
- You encounter undead — what do you know about this type?
- An NPC mentions a deity or cult — what do you know about them?
- You find a religious text — can you interpret it?
- You witness a ritual — do you recognize it?

**In practice:** More useful than you'd think in Forgotten Realms, where gods are very active and cults are common villains. INT-based, so Wizards and Clerics with decent INT get the most from it.

## Wisdom Skills

### Animal Handling (WIS)
**What it covers:** Calming a frightened animal, getting a mount to do something risky, sensing an animal's mood, and controlling domesticated animals.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- Your horse is spooked — can you calm it?
- You approach a guard dog without getting bitten
- You try to ride a horse through dangerous terrain
- You try to befriend a wild animal
- You drive a cart pulled by panicked oxen

**In practice:** Very situational. Comes up most when mounts or animal companions are involved. Rangers, Druids, and Cavalier Fighters get the most use.

### Insight (WIS)
**What it covers:** Reading body language, sensing motives, detecting lies, and understanding the emotional state of a creature. The "gut feeling" skill.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- An NPC tells you something — are they lying? (Your Insight vs. their Deception)
- Someone makes a proposal — what's their real motive?
- You watch a conversation between two NPCs — what's the dynamic? Who has power?
- A party member is acting strangely — do you pick up on it?
- A merchant quotes you a price — are you being ripped off?

**The DM may also use it passively** — if an NPC is lying and your passive Insight is high enough, the DM might just tell you something feels off without you asking.

**In practice:** One of the most frequently used social skills. This is your lie detector, your people-reading tool, your "something's wrong" instinct. Valuable for any character, but especially WIS-based classes (Clerics, Druids, Rangers, Monks, Rogues).

### Medicine (WIS)
**What it covers:** Diagnosing illness, stabilizing a dying creature, and understanding diseases and poisons.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- An ally drops to 0 HP and you don't have healing magic — you can try to stabilize them (DC 10 Medicine check)
- An NPC is sick — what's wrong with them?
- You examine a corpse — what killed them?
- You encounter a poison — can you identify it?

**In practice:** Stabilizing dying allies is the main use, but most parties have a healer who just casts a spell instead. Examining corpses and identifying poisons is niche but flavorful. A healer's kit (5 gp) lets you stabilize automatically without rolling.

### Perception (WIS)
**What it covers:** Noticing things through your senses — seeing, hearing, smelling, feeling. The "are you aware of your surroundings?" skill. The most rolled skill in D&D.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- There's a hidden enemy — do you spot them? (vs. their Stealth)
- There's a trap — do you notice it before triggering it?
- Someone is following you — do you sense them?
- There's a sound behind a door — do you hear it?
- There's a faint smell of poison in your wine — do you notice?
- Something is different about this room — do you pick up on it?

**Passive Perception:** 10 + modifier. This is your "always on" awareness. The DM checks against it constantly without telling you. Most hidden things have a DC of 10-15.

**In practice:** The single most important skill in the game. It keeps you alive (traps, ambushes), drives the story forward (noticing clues), and defines the party's awareness. Rogues and Rangers often take Expertise in Perception. Any WIS-based character benefits enormously from proficiency here.

### Survival (WIS)
**What it covers:** Tracking creatures, navigating wilderness, predicting weather, foraging for food and water, identifying natural hazards, and recognizing signs of nearby creatures.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You find tracks — can you follow them? How old are they? How many creatures?
- You're lost in the wilderness — can you navigate back?
- You need food and water — can you forage?
- You're looking for a specific creature's lair — can you read the environment for signs?
- You want to identify what kind of creature passed through here recently
- You set up camp — can you find a defensible, sheltered position?

**In practice:** Very useful in wilderness and travel-heavy campaigns. Tracking is the standout use — following someone through a city or forest, reading footprints, figuring out how many enemies went through a passage. Rangers, Druids, and any WIS character in an exploration campaign will use this frequently.

## Charisma Skills

### Deception (CHA)
**What it covers:** Lying, misleading, creating false impressions, maintaining a disguise's believability (the disguise kit makes you *look* different; Deception makes you *act* convincingly), and hiding the truth through misdirection.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You tell a guard a false story (your Deception vs. their Insight)
- You maintain a cover identity in conversation
- You try to create a diversion with a false alarm
- You feign injury, death, or emotion
- You try to bluff in a negotiation — pretending you have something you don't
- You're caught somewhere you shouldn't be and need an excuse

**In practice:** The lying skill. Comes up often in social encounters. Essential for Rogues (especially Assassins and Masterminds), Bards, and Warlocks. Low-CHA characters will struggle — if that's you, avoid situations where you need to lie and let the party face handle it.

### Intimidation (CHA)
**What it covers:** Threatening, coercing, or scaring someone into doing what you want through words, body language, or physical presence.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You threaten a prisoner for information
- You stare down a bully to make them back off
- You make a display of force to discourage a fight
- You interrogate a captured enemy

**Note:** Some DMs allow STR-based Intimidation for physically threatening someone (looming over them, breaking something) rather than CHA-based (verbal threats). Ask your DM if this is an option at your table.

**In practice:** Useful for both CHA characters (verbal threats) and STR characters (physical intimidation, with DM approval). Barbarians, Fighters, Warlocks, and Paladins tend to use this most.

### Performance (CHA)
**What it covers:** Entertaining an audience through music, dance, acting, storytelling, or other artistic expression.

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You try to entertain a crowd at a tavern
- You act a part in a play or disguise scenario
- You try to impress someone with artistic skill
- You create a distraction through a performance

**In practice:** Rarely used unless you're a Bard or specifically built for it. The primary Bard skill, and occasionally useful for anyone creating distractions.

### Persuasion (CHA)
**What it covers:** Convincing someone through genuine appeal — logic, charm, good faith, etiquette, or emotional connection. The "honest" social skill (vs. Deception's dishonest approach).

**When the DM calls for it:**
- You negotiate a price with a merchant
- You ask a lord for aid against a common enemy
- You try to calm an angry mob
- You make a case for why someone should trust you
- You appeal to an NPC's emotions or sense of duty

**In practice:** The most common social skill. Every party needs at least one character with decent Persuasion. Bards, Paladins, Sorcerers, and Warlocks are natural fits. If your CHA is low, let the party face handle negotiations.
