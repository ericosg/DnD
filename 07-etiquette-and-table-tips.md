# Etiquette & Table Tips — How to Be a Great Player on Day One

This is the stuff nobody writes on the character sheet but everyone notices. Following these unwritten rules will make your group glad you're at the table.

---

## The Big Five

### 1. Pay Attention When It's Not Your Turn

This is the #1 thing that separates good players from frustrating ones. When the Wizard is negotiating with the merchant, don't zone out on your phone. Why:

- You might hear information your character needs.
- Your turn comes faster than you think — if you weren't listening, the table waits while you ask "what's happening?"
- It's just respectful. Everyone is sharing limited table time.

**Practical tip:** If you tend to drift, keep your hands busy with your dice. Roll them quietly, fidget with them. It keeps you present without needing to be "on."

### 2. Know What You're Doing Before Your Turn

In combat, plan your move while others are taking theirs. When the DM says "your turn," you should already know:

- Where you're moving
- What you're attacking or doing
- Which dice you need

Nothing kills momentum like: "Oh, it's my turn? Uhhh... let me look at my sheet... what can I do again?"

**For your first few sessions:** It's completely fine to be slow while you learn. Everyone expects that. But the effort to stay engaged and be ready is what matters.

### 3. Don't Touch Other People's Dice

This is a weirdly sacred thing in D&D culture. Many players are superstitious about their dice. Don't pick up someone else's dice, don't borrow them without asking, and definitely don't roll them casually.

Bring your own set. If you forget, ask before borrowing.

### 4. Don't Rules-Lawyer

You've been reading these guides. You might know a rule the DM doesn't, or disagree with a ruling. Here's how to handle it:

- **First time:** Mention it briefly. "Hey, I think Sneak Attack works a bit differently — can I show you the text?"
- **If the DM rules differently:** Accept it. Move on. The game continues.
- **After the session:** Bring it up casually. "Hey, I looked up that rule — want to check it for next time?"

The DM's job is hard enough without players arguing about rules mid-combat. They may also be changing rules intentionally for their game.

### 5. Respect Everyone's Fun

Different players enjoy different things:

- Some love deep roleplaying and character voices
- Some love tactical combat optimization
- Some love exploration and puzzle solving
- Some just want to hang out with friends and roll dice

All of these are valid. Don't mock the player doing a funny voice. Don't rush the player who wants to investigate every room. Don't dismiss the player who just wants to hit things. You're all there to have fun, and fun looks different for everyone.

## At the Table

### Your Phone

Put it away or face-down. The single biggest distraction at D&D tables. If you need it for your digital character sheet or spell lookup, that's fine — but close everything else. No texting, no social media, no "just checking one thing."

Your party will notice. Your DM will *definitely* notice.

### Alcohol and Snacks

Many groups drink while playing. That's fine. Just know that D&D requires you to do math, remember details, and make decisions. Buzzed is fun. Drunk is a problem — for you and for the table.

For snacks: keep greasy food away from character sheets and books. Wipe your hands before touching shared materials. This sounds basic but you'd be amazed.

### Bathroom and Breaks

Sessions are long. Get up when you need to. Most groups take a break every 60-90 minutes. If yours doesn't and you need one, just say "can we take five?" Nobody minds.

Good times for a break: after a combat encounter ends, during a travel montage, when the party is discussing plans and your character isn't central to the decision.

### Crosstalk

When the DM is describing something or an NPC is talking, don't have side conversations. Even whispering is distracting. Save it for a natural pause.

If you need to tell another *character* something secretly, pass a note or text them. The DM might even reward this with bonus information.

## Your First Session Specifically

### Things That Are Normal and Expected

- Forgetting what to roll. ("Is that a d8 or a d10?")
- Not knowing what your abilities do yet.
- Being quiet while you figure out the vibe.
- Asking "can I do X?" — this is literally how the game works.
- Rolling terribly. Dice don't care about your preparation.
- Feeling awkward about roleplaying in front of people.

### Things That Will Impress the Table

- Having your character sheet filled out and understanding the basics (you're doing this right now).
- Writing down NPC names without being told to.
- Saying "what does my character know about this?" instead of metagaming.
- Letting other players have their moments.
- Saying "I want to try something — is it possible to...?" Creative thinking is the soul of D&D.
- Being enthusiastic. Even quiet enthusiasm. The table can feel it.

### Things to Avoid

- Trying to "win" D&D. There's no winning. There's surviving, growing, and telling a great story.
- Talking over other players or the DM.
- Insisting on doing everything yourself because your Rogue has the best skills.
- Spending ten minutes on your turn optimizing the perfect move. Good enough is good enough. Speed matters more than perfection.
- Saying "my character wouldn't do that" to avoid cooperating with the party. Find a reason your character *would*.

## Player-to-Player Etiquette

### PvP (Player vs. Player)

Unless your group explicitly agrees, **don't attack other player characters.** Don't steal from them. Don't sabotage them. Even "in character" conflict between PCs should be discussed out of character first.

Your character is a trained assassin. It might be "in character" to steal something from an ally. Don't. It's not fun for the other player. Save the knives for NPCs and monsters.

### Character Secrets

It's fine to have a secret backstory. Many characters do. But don't let secrets become walls between you and the party. A secret is fun when it eventually comes out and creates a great story moment. A secret is toxic when it means you're playing a different game from everyone else.

If the Silent Cord's mission conflicts with the party's goals? That's a *story moment* waiting to happen, not a reason to undermine your friends.

### Consent and Boundaries

Some D&D games go to dark places — torture, horror, betrayal, death. If something at the table makes you genuinely uncomfortable (not your character — *you*), it's okay to say so. Many groups establish boundaries in Session Zero.

If your group doesn't discuss this, and something comes up that bothers you, a simple "hey, can we fade to black on this scene?" is always acceptable.

## Logistics

### Scheduling

The #1 killer of D&D campaigns is scheduling. The game requires 3-6 people to be in the same place for 3-5 hours. Some practical tips:

- Set a **recurring day/time** if possible (every other Saturday, every Tuesday night).
- Respond promptly when the group plans sessions. Don't be the person everyone is waiting on.
- If you can't make a session, say so early. The DM can write your character out for the session.
- Don't cancel last-minute unless it's a real emergency. People set aside their entire evening for this.

### Between Sessions

- **Level up your character** between sessions, not at the table. If you need help, ask the DM or look it up beforehand.
- **Review your notes.** Five minutes before the next session, skim what happened last time. You'll be sharper than most of the table.
- **Think about what your character wants to do next.** Not a rigid plan — just a direction. "I want to investigate the thieves' guild" or "I want to find better weapons."
- **Don't pester the DM** with questions between sessions unless they've said they're open to it. They're doing a lot of prep already.

## The Secret Cheat Code

Here it is, the one thing that makes you an amazing D&D player:

> **Be someone everyone is excited to play with.**

That means:
- Show up on time.
- Be prepared.
- Pay attention.
- Be generous with the spotlight.
- Celebrate other players' cool moments.
- Roll with the unexpected.
- Thank the DM after the session. They put in hours of prep for your entertainment.

You don't need to know every rule. You don't need the optimal build. You don't need a dramatic backstory. You just need to be engaged, cooperative, and enthusiastic. The rest comes with time.
